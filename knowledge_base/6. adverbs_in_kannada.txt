A Grammar Guide to Kannada Adverbs
(The "-āgi" Rule)
1. The "Magic Suffix": -āgi (-ಆಗಿ)
In English, we often turn an Adjective into an Adverb by adding -ly (Quick -> Quickly).
In Kannada, we do the exact same thing by adding -āgi (-ಆಗಿ) to a Noun or Adjective.
Origin: This suffix comes from the past participle of ಆಗು (Āgu - to become).
Literal Logic: "Having become [Adjective], do [Action]."
Sandhi Rules: Just like with Adjectives, we insert a glide letter to connect the words:
If the word ends in -a, add -v- (vāgi).
If the word ends in -i or -e, add -y- (yāgi).

2. The "How Are You?" Exception
This is a critical conversational point.
In English, "I am well" is the grammatically correct way to describe your health or state, though "I am good" is extremely common in casual conversation.
Kannada strictly enforces the "Well" logic. You cannot use the adjective "Good" (Chenna) to describe your state; you must use the adverb "Well" (Chennāgi).
Question: Hēgidīra? (How are you?)
Wrong Answer: Nānu chenna iddīni. (I am good person existing.)
Correct Answer: ನಾನು ಚೆನ್ನಾಗಿ ಇದ್ದೀನಿ. (Nānu chennāgi iddīni.)
Literal Meaning: I am existing in a fine way / I am doing well.
Note: If you are describing an object (like a movie or food), you use the Adjective + Āgide rule.
Coffee chennāgide. (The coffee is good).
Nānu chennāgi iddīni. (I am well).

Not all adverbs need the -āgi suffix. Some words are born as adverbs.

These are crucial for storytelling (sequencing events).

ಅವನು ಯಾವಾಗಲೂ ತಡವಾಗಿ ಬರುತ್ತಾನೆ. (He always comes late.)

5. Turning Nouns into Feelings with -āgi
You can use -āgi to describe how you feel physically or emotionally.
Noun: Husu (Smile/Lightness) -> Husiyāgi (Smilingly).
Noun: Sustu (Tiredness) -> Sustāgi (Tiredly).
Example: Nanage sustāgi ide. (It is existing tiredly to me -> I feel tired).
Noun: Huchchu (Madness) -> Huchchāgi (Madly).
Example: Avaru huchchāgi preetistāre. (They love madly).
6. Summary Checklist
When speaking, ask yourself:
Am I describing a Thing (Noun)? -> Use Adjective (e.g., Doḍḍa mane).
Am I describing an Action (Verb)? -> Use -āgi (e.g., Jōrāgi mātanāḍu).
Am I saying "I am well"? -> Use Chennāgi (not Chenna).
